# 🧠 Concept: Ad Optimization Systems

This guide describes the mathematical modeling and optimization techniques used in ad-tech systems to optimize bids, allocate budgets, and select creatives under business constraints.

---

## 1. Bid Optimization (Utility Maximization)
In real-time bidding (RTB), an advertiser must determine the optimal bid price $b$ for an ad impression to maximize utility (e.g. expected conversions) while managing costs.

### Mathematical Formulation
Let:
*   $v$ be the estimated value of the impression (e.g., $v = \text{eCTR} \times \text{Conversion Value}$).
*   $P_{\text{win}}(b)$ be the probability of winning the auction with a bid of $b$.
*   $Cost(b)$ be the expected cost given a win (under first-price or second-price auction mechanics).

For a **first-price auction**, if you win, you pay your bid $b$. The expected utility $U(b)$ is:
$$U(b) = (v - b) \times P_{\text{win}}(b)$$

The objective is to find $b^*$ that maximizes $U(b)$:
$$b^* = \arg\max_{b} \left[ (v - b) \times P_{\text{win}}(b) \right]$$

In practice, $P_{\text{win}}(b)$ is modeled as a cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the market price (e.g. using a log-normal or survival model).

## 2. Budget Allocation (Linear Programming)
Advertisers often need to distribute a total daily budget $B$ across multiple ad campaigns or targeting channels $i \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ to maximize total conversions.

### Mathematical Formulation
Let:
*   $x_i \ge 0$ be the budget allocated to campaign $i$.
*   $r_i(x_i)$ be the expected revenue/conversions generated by campaign $i$ as a function of its allocated budget.
*   $c_i$ be the cost per conversion in campaign $i$.

If we assume linear returns $r_i(x_i) = \alpha_i x_i$ for simplicity within bounded ranges, we can formulate this as a **Linear Programming (LP)** problem:

$$\text{Maximize} \quad \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i x_i$$
$$\text{Subject to} \quad \sum_{i=1}^N x_i \le B$$
$$0 \le x_i \le M_i \quad \forall i$$
where $M_i$ is the maximum spend capacity of campaign $i$. This can be solved efficiently using simplex or interior-point methods.

## 3. Creative Selection & Multi-Armed Bandits (MAB)
To select the best ad creative for a given audience, we face an **Exploration vs. Exploitation** dilemma. We must display high-performing creatives (exploit) while testing newer ones (explore).

### Multi-Armed Bandit Formulations
Each creative $a$ is an "arm" with an unknown click-through rate (CTR) $\theta_a$.

#### A. Thompson Sampling (Bayesian Approach)
1.  Assume a Beta prior distribution for each creative's CTR: $\theta_a \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_a, \beta_a)$.
2.  For each auction/decision point:
    *   Sample $\tilde{\theta}_a \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_a, \beta_a)$ for all arms.
    *   Select the creative $a^*$ with the highest sampled value: $a^* = \arg\max_{a} \tilde{\theta}_a$.
3.  Observe user feedback (Click $Y=1$ or No Click $Y=0$).
4.  Update the parameters for arm $a^*$:
    *   If click: $\alpha_{a^*} \leftarrow \alpha_{a^*} + 1$
    *   If no click: $\beta_{a^*} \leftarrow \beta_{a^*} + 1$

#### B. Upper Confidence Bound (UCB1)
Select the arm that maximizes:
$$a^* = \arg\max_{a} \left[ \bar{\mu}_a + c \sqrt{\frac{\ln t}{N_a(t)}} \right]$$
where $\bar{\mu}_a$ is the historical CTR of creative $a$, $t$ is the total trials, $N_a(t)$ is the number of times creative $a$ was selected, and $c$ controls the exploration rate.
